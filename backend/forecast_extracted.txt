This report provides a 12–24 month security technology forecast for Walmart’s enterprise security function, focused on emerging capabilities that can materially reduce risk across retail and adjacent environments. It is designed for decision-makers (Directors → CSO) and emphasizes: (1) implementation realism, (2) measurable outcomes, and (3) privacy/legal defensibility.

Executive Summary 
This Q1 Forecast Report is an awareness briefing for Walmart Global Security, Aviation, & Investigations leadership on the security technology landscape most likely to matter over the next 12–24 months. It is intentionally vendor-agnostic and emphasizes what is deployable at Walmart scale, what is high-risk/high-sensitivity, and what requires governance maturity before operational use.

Cross-cutting constraints 
Operational viability beats model accuracy. At Walmart scale, false positives drive labor cost, customer impact, and loss of trust; “good enough” detection without disciplined operations typically fails in production.
Governance is a prerequisite, not a follow-on. Any capability that triggers intervention, detention/escalation, or impacts a customer transaction requires defensible ground truth, audit trails, and review pathways.
Privacy and biometrics risk must be explicitly managed. Where biometric inference is contemplated, treat it as a separate risk tier requiring privacy-by-design, retention minimization, redaction strategies, and jurisdictional controls.
Integration patterns should be standardized from the start. This portfolio will succeed faster if we normalize around:

SharePoint-based evidence/log repositories (or equivalent approved internal repositories) for investigation artifacts, program documentation, and audit-ready records.

Operating Context (12–24 month horizon, threat + shrink convergence)
Over the next 12–24 months, Walmart’s highest-leverage security gains come from treating the store as a cyber-physical system—where shrink, ORC, fraud, insider risk, and safety incidents share the same dependency: trusted telemetry + defensible evidence pipelines that can drive action without creating legal/brand blowback.
1) “Store-as-Anchor” for Converged Security
Stores remain the operational center of gravity because they are where physical loss, customer experience, employee safety, and brand risk converge in real time. The near-term pattern is not “more cameras” or “more sensors” in isolation—it’s integration: fusing POS/transaction logs, video analytics, RFID/EPCIS events, and incident reporting into a single operational picture that can support both prevention and prosecution-quality documentation.Image 2 — Store-as-Anchor: Cyber-Physical Telemetry → Action → Defensible Evidence

Your Phase 1 work already points to the practical reality: shrink programs increasingly depend on log-derived detection (voids, refunds, discount abuse, exception events, SCO behaviors) and correlation (same actor/time/register/product patterns) rather than single-point signals. That implies a future where the primary “sensor” is often the data exhaust of retail operations—and where the security differentiator is whether that exhaust is trustworthy, normalized, and reviewable at scale. 
2) Threat + Shrink Convergence: What’s Actually Converging
In the 12–24 month window, “convergence” becomes tangible in three ways:
A. ORC + fraud behave like cyber campaigns (repeatable, adaptive, multi-channel).Returns fraud strategies highlight that adversaries iterate quickly against policy, identity, and controls—pushing Walmart toward risk-based decisioning (dynamic thresholds, entity resolution, network/graph link analysis) while constantly managing customer friction/false positives as a first-order constraint. 
B. Retail CV and sensor fusion will push security decisions closer to the edge.The CV deployment assessment is explicit that practical deployments optimize for edge inference to control latency/bandwidth and to reduce uncontrolled PII spillage—while still requiring an enterprise-grade path for metadata, alerting, and audit trails. It also calls out the hard problem: moving from “detection” to “intervention” increases liability dramatically (misidentification, bias, false detention), especially as capabilities expand from object detection into behaviors and person-centric analytics. 
C. Supply chain traceability becomes a security domain, not just compliance.RFID/EPCIS and Digital Product Passport readiness work shows a clear pull toward event-level provenance (what happened, where, when, by whom/what system) and cross-entity data sharing. That has immediate security implications: event integrity, exception handling, and tamper evidence become core controls—while competitive sensitivity and privacy boundaries become harder as traceability requirements expand globally. 
3) “Telemetry is the Product” (and Evidence is the Liability)
Across these domains, the deciding factor is whether Walmart can treat telemetry as admissible and operationally actionable.
Incident reporting + BWC metadata work reinforces that the evidence lifecycle is not optional overhead: chain-of-custody, retention, redaction, and auditability determine whether technology reduces risk or becomes a discovery/PR accelerant.

4) Identity, Access, and “Who Did What” Must Be Standardized
As new security technologies proliferate (CV platforms, ORC analytics, incident evidence systems, robotics telemetry, traceability platforms), the consistent weak point is usually identity and audit.
Minimum standard for this report’s horizon:
Entra ID as the control plane for access (SSO + Conditional Access + least privilege + break-glass discipline).
Centralized audit trails: every material event (alert creation, evidence export, policy override, model/version changes) must produce immutable logs.
Operationally consumable logging to SharePoint (structured, searchable, retention-aligned), with a clear path to downstream analytics/SIEM as needed.
Passkey/MFA telemetry work supports the direction: phishing-resistant auth and strong telemetry are not “IT hygiene”; they are prerequisite controls for preventing account takeover and insider misuse in the toolchain that will run these programs.

Reference Architecture (Entra ID integration patterns + SharePoint logging model)
For emerging security tech to be a force multiplier at Walmart scale, we need a repeatable reference architecture that standardizes identity, auditability, and evidence handling—so new capabilities can be evaluated and adopted without creating hidden privacy, litigation, or integrity risks.
Architecture intent (vendor-agnostic)
This reference architecture is not a backend implementation spec. It is a minimum integration standard we can apply across technology categories (CV, fraud analytics, RFID/EPCIS, bot defense, OT telemetry, incident/evidence platforms) to validate whether a solution is operationally viable and risk-contained.
Design goals:
Identity-first access control for people, services, and integrations.
End-to-end auditability (who did what, when, from where, and with what data/model/version).
Defensible evidence chain for investigations (immutability + provenance + retention discipline).
Privacy-by-design defaults, with explicit gating for high-sensitivity use cases.
Portability/exit strategy (avoid single-vendor cloud lock-in without a roadmap).

Privacy-preserving logging defaults: Use pseudonymization for analytics/trending and minimize logged attributes to what’s needed for the security decision; this is explicitly recommended for identity telemetry and generalizes cleanly to other categories.

4) High-sensitivity lanes Some categories cross a risk boundary where “normal” logging and storage practices become insufficient:
Biometrics/person-centric analytics (highest privacy + litigation risk)
BWC/video evidence and incident records

Purpose limitation + access attestation

Model/label chain-of-custody (label audit trails, drift monitoring, and retention of “model pedigree” artifacts)
Retention model 
Image 5 — Retention & Legal Hold Model: Utility With Minimized Exposure

Walmart’s preference to retain data should be implemented as tiered retention that retains utility while minimizing unnecessary exposure:
Hot (30–90 days): full-fidelity logs for active IR/forensics.
Cold (1–2 years): summarized/anonymized trend data; purge detailed logs automatically unless held. 
Legal holds: explicit triggers for incident/evidence workflows (e.g., severe events, anticipated litigation) with documented handling paths (this becomes critical for spoliation risk).
Why this matters to Walmart Emerging security tech succeeds or fails on governance and integration, not demos. A standardized Entra ID + SharePoint-centric reference architecture lets us scale “what works” across stores and expand globally while reducing two existential risks: (1) actions without defensible audit/evidence, and (2) privacy/biometric exposure created by unmanaged data flows.

Identity Hardening & Telemetry (Passkeys/MFA, fraud reduction, privacy)
Image 6 — Identity as Force Multiplier: Passkey-First + Fraud Suppression Linkage

Over the next 12–24 months, identity becomes the highest-ROI “force multiplier” control: shifting high-value flows to phishing-resistant auth (passkeys/FIDO2) and instrumenting method-level telemetry enables measurable risk reduction while directly suppressing fraud patterns (ATO, bot-driven abuse, synthetic identity rings)—if we implement privacy-preserving logging and strict data minimization.
1) Why identity is the control plane for both workforce and customer risk
Identity compromise is no longer an “IT-only” problem. It is an enabler for:
Workforce compromise → privileged access misuse, ransomware footholds, and lateral movement (the classic “phish → pivot” chain).
Customer identity abuse → account takeover (ATO), loyalty/gift card theft, high-velocity returns abuse, and policy exploitation. Returns fraud patterns explicitly note bot networks and distributed “mule” operations that avoid velocity checks tied to a single identity. 
The practical implication: we should treat identity controls as loss prevention + cyber resilience infrastructure, not a siloed IAM initiative.
2) Target state: passkey-first where impact is highest
The forecast signal in the data is that “middle-ground” MFA (SMS and basic push) is eroding, with organizations bifurcating into low-friction low-security for low-value flows, and high-friction high-security (FIDO2) for workforce and high-value transactions. 
Recommended posture (vendor-agnostic):
Privileged workforce: passkeys/FIDO2 as the default target state (aim for “effectively 100%” coverage).
Broad workforce: passkeys prioritized for high-frequency logins and high-impact apps (where friction reduction also returns productivity).
Customer/high-value actions: passkeys for actions that monetize fraud when compromised (change shipping details, payment updates, loyalty redemptions, high-risk returns flows). The passkey-first trend is accelerating as major consumer platforms normalize passkey usage, reducing adoption friction.

OT Robotics Telemetry Security 
In automated DCs and robotics-heavy facilities, cybersecurity incidents can become physical safety events and operational downtime; the winning posture is IEC 62443-style zones/conduits + protocol-aware controls + fused “cyber + physics” telemetry that can distinguish real failures from adversarial manipulation.
1) Operating context: IT/OT convergence with real-world consequences
Modern distribution and fulfillment environments blend IT systems (WMS, identity, analytics) with OT/IIoT systems (WCS, fleet managers, AMRs/ASRS, safety PLCs, sortation). This collapses traditional security boundaries: an intrusion that begins in IT can pivot into control systems where the impact is not just data loss, but inventory destruction, equipment damage, or human injury—and “safe-state” behaviors can be exploited for denial-of-service via repeated shutdowns. The security objective is therefore operational continuity + safety integrity, not merely confidentiality.
2) Reference architecture: move from Purdue assumptions to IEC 62443 zones and conduits
A classic Purdue hierarchy breaks down with mobile robots, wireless links, and east–west traffic patterns. The more defensible model is zones and conduits:
Image 24 — OT Robotics Security Reference Architecture: IEC 62443 Zones & Conduits

Recommended zones
Zone A — Safety Systems (highest criticality): safety PLCs, safety interlocks, E-stops, safety-rated sensors.
Zone B — Robotic/Mobile Assets: AMRs/AGVs, robot cells, local controllers, safety-rated motion controllers.
Zone C — Control Orchestration: fleet management, WCS, robotic supervisors, local OT management consoles.
Zone D — Business/IT Systems: WMS, enterprise services, analytics, identity services, ticketing.
Zone E — OEM/Remote Support (tightly controlled): vendor diagnostics and support channels (ideally brokered, time-bound, and fully audited).
Conduits must be explicit and minimized
Fleet Command Conduit (C↔B): command + telemetry; enforce mTLS, device identity, and command validation (no “any robot can talk to any topic/endpoint”).
Safety Interlock Conduit (B↔A): safety protocols only; isolate and protect from congestion/DoS; latency monitoring is a control.
Business Logic Conduit (D↔C): orders/inventory; protect with API gateways/WAF-equivalent controls and strict authZ.
OEM Access Conduit (E↔C, rarely ↔B): brokered access with session recording, approvals, and rapid revoke.
3) Protocol controls: “OT-aware” enforcement, not generic firewalls
Robotics/DC environments mix deterministic OT protocols and IT/IoT protocols; controls must understand protocol semantics:
PROFINET / EtherNet/IP (CIP): monitor and restrict discovery/config traffic; enforce write-command controls (e.g., block/alert on unauthorized “set attribute”/write services).
Modbus TCP and legacy write functions: tightly control or eliminate write paths outside authorized controllers.
Safety protocols (CIP Safety / PROFIsafe / Safety over EtherCAT): protect timing and availability; treat unexpected latency/bursts as a safety and security event.
MQTT/IoT pub-sub: enforce topic topology per device (robot publishes only to its own status topics, subscribes only to its own command topics); validate payloads at the broker or through inspection points to reduce command-injection risk.
Best-practice baseline: default-deny between zones, allowlist by device identity + protocol + command class, and implement protocol DPI where feasible.
4) Monitoring: fuse network telemetry with “physics + logic” telemetry
Image 25 — High-Confidence Tripwires: Fusing Network + Physics + Controller Integrity With Safe Containment

The most reliable detection in cyber-physical systems comes from correlating:
Network behavior: who talks to whom, protocol misuse, port scanning, anomalous bandwidth or east–west expansion.
Physical/process signals: motor current/torque anomalies, joint velocity violations, abnormal battery discharge/temperature patterns, IMU inconsistencies (“robot claims stationary, IMU shows movement”), odometry vs Lidar/vision mismatches (sensor spoofing/blinding).
Control logic/state integrity: PLC scan-time drift, unexpected memory map changes, evidence of I/O forcing, and “gold image” fingerprint deviation in controller memory/state.
Design goal: build “high-confidence tripwires” where independent signals agree (e.g., suspicious network session to fleet manager + IMU disagreement + command out-of-policy), enabling automated containment that is safe (isolate robot, degrade to safe mode, require human reset) rather than brittle blocking.
5) Safety-stop abuse: treat spurious trips as a cyber vector
Safety systems are designed to fail safe—attackers can exploit that by inducing repeated false safety stops (DoS). The mitigation is not to weaken safety; it’s to add root-cause discrimination:
physics consistency checks (inertia vs reported velocity spikes)
cross-sensor disagreement detection (Lidar vs camera vs ultrasonics)
probabilistic models of valid state transitions (abnormal transition sequences indicate logic anomalies)
engineering controls like 2-out-of-3 voting for diverse sensors where appropriate, plus debouncing/filters that don’t violate safety constraints
6) Vulnerability and asset governance: SBOM + virtual patching + passive inventory
Robotics stacks are supply-chain dense (OS/RTOS, ROS/ROS2/DDS, drivers, CV libraries). The defensible model is:
SBOM ingestion mapped to affected asset inventory
risk-based patching keyed to exploitability and zone criticality
virtual patching at conduits (protocol-aware rules) when patching is operationally constrained
passive discovery and OT asset taxonomy (safety controller vs HMI vs robot vs sensor) to avoid disrupting fragile equipment
Integration notes for the report
Identity control: enforce role-based access and privileged workflows via Entra ID patterns for human/admin access; broker OEM access with time-bound approvals and full audit.
Logging/governance: store governance artifacts, zone/conduit definitions, exception approvals, and investigation summaries in SharePoint; retain high-fidelity telemetry short-term with longer-term summarized trends (tiered retention).
Vendor posture: prefer platforms that can operate without AWS dependency or provide a credible roadmap off it, aligned to Walmart data-retention expectations.
Why this matters to Walmart:OT robotics security is a force multiplier: it protects uptime, safety, and shrink simultaneously. The failure mode is asymmetric—attackers can cause costly downtime or physical disruption without “stealing data.” Zones/conduits, protocol-aware enforcement, and fused cyber/physics telemetry create a defensible posture that scales with automation growth while preserving safety integrity.

Drone Detection Feasibility (detection vs mitigation, legal constraints)
Counter-UAS for Walmart is feasible only as detection + intelligence, not as active defeat. The business value is in log-derived early warning (pre-incident reconnaissance signals) and defensible evidence handling—provided we operate inside strict federal/state constraints, avoid “pilot tracking” overreach, and run a disciplined, low-noise pilot before investing in multi-sensor systems.
1) What problem we’re actually solving (12–24 months)
For retail logistics hubs (DCs/FCs) and select high-risk store-adjacent sites, drones create three practical risk pillars:
Reconnaissance / pre-operational surveillance: mapping trailer yards, reading labels, observing patrol rhythms, identifying weak points. This is the highest-probability “real world” adversary use case.
Operational disruption / safety: nuisance flights, collisions, or deliberate interference that can trigger stoppages and create injury risk.
Insider enablement / collusion: drone presence as a signaling mechanism or micro-drop/collection vector.
Key insight: Walmart does not need to “win the air war.” Walmart needs airspace awareness that turns drones into a leading indicator for theft attempts and operational risk.
2) The bright line: detection vs mitigation
Image 26 — Counter-UAS Bright Line: Detection + Intelligence Only (No Active Defeat)
Best-judgment legal posture for private retail:
Detection, tracking, alerting, and evidence capture are feasible if designed carefully.
Mitigation/defeat is not. Any “soft kill,” jamming, spoofing/takeover, netting, or kinetic defeat marketed to private entities should be treated as a red-flag vendor claim and escalated to Legal immediately.
Operational implication: Our response model is detect → raise posture → protect assets → notify appropriate parties (security operations + local law enforcement where needed). Not “interdict.”
3) Technical feasibility: what works in real retail environments
Retail logistics sites are hard environments: high RF noise, complex structures, line-of-sight constraints, and frequent false positives if sensors are poorly placed. Single-sensor solutions are brittle. The feasible architecture is sensor fusion with a pragmatic progression:
Modality A — Remote ID receivers (low cost, high yield, incomplete coverage)
Best for compliant/cooperative drones and establishing baseline awareness.
Limitation: adversaries can fly “non-cooperative/dark” drones (disabled RID, legacy/home-built).
Modality B — RF detection (broad coverage, legal nuance)
Effective for detecting the presence of common drone control links.
Best practice is spectrum/fingerprint detection without decoding content to avoid wiretap-type concerns.
Modality C — Radar (coverage for “dark” drones, false positive management required)
Valuable for non-cooperative drones, but can generate high nuisance tracks (birds, vehicles, rotating equipment).
Requires careful tuning and ideally “two-sensor confirmation” logic to keep alert volume manageable.
Modality D — EO/IR cameras (classification and evidence, not primary detection)
Used to visually confirm and capture evidence once cued by RF/radar/RID.
Feasibility takeaway: For Walmart-scale deployments, the realistic target is RID + RF first, then RF + radar + PTZ only at Tier-1 sites with a demonstrated threat level or high-value exposure.
4) Log analytics: the value is in patterns, not single alerts
The strongest ROI comes from operationalizing detection logs into pre-incident intelligence and targeted posture changes.
Log fields to treat as first-class

Altitude bands (high altitude transit vs low altitude yard inspection)

Practical “kill chain” indicators

Repeated nightly recurrence at consistent times
Long dwell (>30 seconds) over specific assets (dock doors, high-value trailer rows)
“Overwatch” behavior aligned with known theft windows
Security operations use: these indicators trigger a proactive response (temporary patrol adjustments, yard hardening actions, controlled asset movement) rather than waiting for the theft attempt.
5) Legal and privacy constraints 
This program can create a “toxic asset” if mishandled. The constraints must be explicit:
Signal interception constraints
Avoid systems that decode payload content (e.g., video feed demodulation, command decoding).
Prefer detection that measures presence/fingerprint and uses Remote ID broadcasts where appropriate.
Pilot tracking and privacy
Remote ID can expose controller/pilot location. Treat this as high-risk data:
minimize collection where not required
restrict access tightly
do not track individuals beyond the immediate facility boundary as a default posture
never use for public identification or “shaming”
share externally only via controlled law-enforcement/legal workflows
State variability
Privacy/trespass regimes vary materially by state; implement a policy posture that is conservative by default and configurable by jurisdiction.
Core rule of thumb
Detect the drone (object). Be cautious about “tracking the person” as an analytics product.
6) Retention and legal holds
Best-judgment retention model that preserves value without creating unnecessary discovery exposure:
Operational logs (non-incident): retain 30–90 days for trend analysis and heatmapping; automatically purge on schedule.
Incident-tagged events: if a flight correlates to theft, intrusion, safety event, or credible threat:
promote the record to Digital Evidence handling
enforce chain-of-custody (sealed manifests, access logging)
retain per legal guidance and local statutes/limitations (often multi-year)
Derivatives over raw: retain structured metadata and analytic summaries longer; keep raw/high-precision sensitive artifacts under tighter access controls.
This section is intentionally consistent with S12: evidence must be sealed, auditable, and export-controlled once it becomes incident material.

1) Portfolio-level synthesis 
- The store is the anchor of convergence: shrink, ORC, customer abuse, insider risk, and safety incidents increasingly share the same technical dependency—high-confidence telemetry that supports action.

4) Cross-cutting risks that require leadership attention (and mitigations expected)
- False positives and alert fatigue (labor cost + trust erosion). Require alert budgets; enforce friction-first ladders; demand reason codes; use fusion to increase confidence before escalating.
- Privacy and biometric exposure (regulatory, class-action, brand harm). Default to non-biometric analytics; gate high-sensitivity lanes through Legal/Compliance; minimize and tier retention; require redaction workflows.
- Evidence integrity failures (prosecution impact, discovery risk, repudiation). Standardize evidence packages, manifests/hashes, immutable access logs, and repeatable legal-hold triggers; avoid editing-in-place.
- Vendor lock-in or misaligned cloud dependencies. Require credible portability/exit plans; demand exportable audit logs and governance artifacts; validate cloud dependencies and roadmap constraints early.
- Model/decision drift and opaque automation. Institutionalize labeling governance, version control, performance monitoring, and retraining/audit triggers; ensure humans can reconstruct 'why' from decision files.
- IT/OT convergence producing safety and availability events. Implement zones/conduits; protocol-aware controls; fused cyber+physics monitoring; safe containment playbooks.

This report is intentionally pragmatic: it prioritizes deployable patterns over aspirational platforms, and it treats governance as the prerequisite for scale. If leadership aligns on the reference architecture minimums, sensitivity gates, and a small KPI slate, Emerging Security Technology can evaluate categories quickly, scale what is operationally viable, and avoid creating new privacy/evidence liabilities while reducing shrink, fraud, and safety risk across Walmart’s converged retail environment.
